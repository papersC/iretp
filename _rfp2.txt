1.   Executive Summary 
The Dubai Land Department (DLD) hereby issues this Request for Proposal (RFP) for the design, 
development, deployment, maintenance, and post-go-live support of the Integrated Real Estate 
Transparency Platform (IRETP). The primary strategic objective is to advance Dubai's standing in the 
JLL Global Real Estate Transparency Index (GRETI) and to reinforce investor confidence among 
international financial institutions, sovereign wealth funds, and global investors. 
 
The platform directly supports the Dubai Economic Agenda D33 and the Smart Dubai Strategy by 
providing open, accurate, real-time, and interactive real estate data to the public while equipping DLD 
leadership with powerful internal risk management and developer oversight tools. 
 
The IRETP comprises two integrated pillars: 
• External Public Portal — A world-class, multilingual data portal for international investors, 
financial institutions, property developers, and the general public, offering interactive 
dashboards, a GIS map, an AI-powered data agent, self-service analytics, and open-data 
export capabilities. 
• Internal Management Platform — A secure, role-based platform for DLD staff and 
leadership, encompassing a developer performance rating engine, Escrow account 
monitoring, an Early Warning Risk System (EWRS), and multi-level leadership alert 
workflows. 
 
This RFP does not prescribe specific technology brands, frameworks, programming languages, or 
hosting providers. Vendors are expected to propose the solution architecture, technology stack, and 
hosting model that best meets the requirements described herein. Proposals are evaluated on 70% 
technical merit and 30% budget and total cost of ownership. 
 
Delivery is structured across four phases, all of which must be completed within 3 calendar months 
from the date of contract signing. Go-live of any phase is conditional on the successful completion of 
all mandatory testing and acceptance criteria for that phase — no phase may go live before its tests 
are fully passed. Following the go-live of the final phase, the vendor must provide a 12-month 
warranty and technical support period as part of this contract.

2.   Project Overview & Strategic Context 
2.1   Background 
The JLL Global Real Estate Transparency Index (GRETI), published biennially, is the primary 
reference used by institutional investors, lenders, and multinational corporations to evaluate real 
estate market quality globally. The 2024 edition assessed 256 individual indicators across 89 
countries and 151 cities. Markets in the 'Highly Transparent' tier attract over 80% of global direct 
commercial real estate investment. Dubai has demonstrated consistent improvement, and this 
platform is designed to accelerate that progress.

5.3   AI Model Architecture — Multi-Model Approach 
DLD does not prescribe a specific AI model, vendor, or provider. The platform must support a multi-
model AI architecture that gives DLD the flexibility to use more than one AI model — either 
simultaneously for different tasks, or switchable by the platform administrator without requiring 
redevelopment. The vendor must propose and justify their multi-model architecture in the proposal. 
 
The following requirements apply to the AI architecture: 
 
AI Architecture 
Requirement Specification 
Multi-Model Support The platform must support the use of more than one AI language model. The 
vendor must design an orchestration layer (e.g., an AI routing or gateway 
component) that sits between the AI Agent and the underlying models, enabling 
DLD to: (a) use different models for different task types (e.g., one model for data 
queries, another for service navigation), (b) switch the active model from the 
administration interface without code changes or redeployment, and (c) add a 
new AI model provider in the future by configuration, not by rebuilding the AI 
layer. 
Acceptable Model Options The AI orchestration layer must be compatible with, and the proposal must 
demonstrate how it will support, a range of model types including but not limited 
to: managed AI API services (such as OpenAI API, Anthropic API, Google 
Gemini API, or equivalent), self-hosted open-source LLMs (such as Llama, 
Mistral, or equivalent models hosted within the UAE), and custom fine-tuned 
models trained on DLD domain data. The vendor may propose any combination 
of these. DLD does not mandate a specific model or provider. 
Mandatory RAG Layer A Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) layer is mandatory regardless of 
which AI model is used. The RAG layer must index the DLD transaction 
database, project registry, developer profiles, RERA regulations, and DLD 
service procedures, and must ground every AI Agent response in retrieved data

Model Fallback & 
Redundancy 
If the primary AI model is unavailable (API outage, service degradation), the 
system must automatically fall back to a secondary model without user -visible 
disruption. The vendor must describe the fallback model and the expected impact 
on response quality during fallback. The AI Agent must never display a raw API 
error to the end user. 
UAE Data Residency All AI inference and data processing must occur within the United Arab 
Emirates, regardless of which AI model is active. If using a managed AI API 
service, the vendor must confirm that the provider offers a UAE or Gulf-region 
inference endpoint and that data does not transit outside the UAE. If no UAE 
endpoint is available for a proposed model, the vendor must propose a compliant 
alternative. 
Custom Fine-Tuning Option The vendor must indicate whether they propose custom fine-tuning of one or 
more models on DLD domain data (real estate terminology, RERA procedures, 
zone nomenclature, transaction classification). If fine-tuning is proposed, the 
vendor must describe the training data approach, the fine-tuning schedule, and 
how model accuracy will be validated post-fine-tuning. Fine-tuning is not 
mandatory but will be evaluated as a differentiator. 
Model Performance 
Transparency 
The administration interface must display, for each configured model: the model 
name and version, the current active/inactive status, the average response latency 
over the last 7 days, and any active fallback conditions. DLD administrators 
must be able to view this information without vendor assistance.

11.4.2   Visualisation Layer — External Public Portal 
While DLD’s internal reporting layer within the Microsoft Fabric ecosystem may utilise Power BI for

semantic models, maintaining the Gold layer as the preferred single source of truth and 
avoiding unnecessary data duplication.

public user volumes, anonymous access capability, UI/UX customisation depth, performance 
at scale (concurrent users), and long-term maintainability.

data APIs.

12.2   Pre-Publication Analytics Assessment 
Before any data is made live on the External Portal, the vendor must complete a Pre-Publication 
Analytics Assessment. This assessment is a mandatory gate — no public data publication is permitted 
until the assessment is completed and signed off by the DLD data liaison officer. The assessment 
must validate: 
• End-to-End Data Reconciliation: platform figures match the authoritative DLD source system 
records for a minimum sample of 500 transactions drawn randomly across all property types, 
zones, and transaction types. Accuracy rate must be 99.5% or above. 
• Derived Metric Validation: every derived metric (price per sqft averages, rental yield %, 
zone-level aggregates, YoY change figures, Escrow adequacy ratios, developer scores) must 
be independently validated from raw data. Any discrepancy must be resolved before go-live. 
• Edge Case & Outlier Review: all statistical outliers must be identified, flagged to DLD, and 
handled (included, excluded, or annotated) by agreement before go-live. 
• Zone Boundary Verification: zone-level data aggregations use the correct official zone 
boundary definitions. No transactions double-counted or misassigned. 
• Trend Direction Validation: all trend indicators (up/stable/down arrows, YoY % changes) 
verified against raw data for correct direction and magnitude.

19.   Data Governance, Privacy & Ownership 
19.1   Data Ownership 
• All data processed, stored, or generated within the IRETP platform is the sole and exclusive 
property of Dubai Land Department. 
• The vendor has no rights to use any DLD data for any purpose other than delivering the 
contracted services. 
• Upon contract expiration, termination, or handover, the vendor must deliver all data in 
machine-readable, open formats within 30 calendar days and provide written certification of 
complete data destruction from all vendor-controlled systems within 60 days. 
 
19.2   Personal Data Protection Compliance 
• The system must fully comply with UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45 of 2021 on the 
Protection of Personal Data (PDPL). 
• No personally identifiable information (PII) — names, Emirates ID, nationality, or contact 
details — may be displayed in any public-facing interface. 
• All transaction data displayed publicly must be either aggregated (zone/period level) or 
anonymised at the individual transaction level. 
• A Data Processing Agreement (DPA) must be signed between DLD and the vendor before 
any personal data is accessed. 
• User consent management for newsletters, alert emails, and cross-session AI memory must 
comply with PDPL.

21.   Glossary of Terms 
Term / Acronym Definition 
CAPEX Capital Expenditure — one-time investment costs incurred during the project 
development and delivery phase. 
DESC Dubai Electronic Security Center — the government authority responsible for 
cybersecurity regulation and certification in the Emirate of Dubai. 
DESC-CSP DESC Cloud Service Provider Certification — mandatory certification for any cloud 
provider or colocation facility serving Dubai Government entities. 
EWRS Early Warning Risk System — the internal platform module (Phase 2) that monitors 
risk indicators for real estate projects and developers and triggers automated alerts. 
GRETI Global Real Estate Transparency Index — published biennially by JLL & LaSalle, 
covering 89 countries and 151 cities across 256 indicators in 6 sub-indices. 
GIS Geographic Information System — technology for capturing, storing, managing, and 
visualising spatially referenced data on digital maps. 
IaC Infrastructure as Code — the practice of managing and provisioning infrastructure 
through machine-readable definition files rather than manual processes. 
IRETP Integrated Real Estate Transparency Platform — the name of the system being 
procured through this RFP. 
ISR Information Security Regulation — the mandatory information security framework 
issued by DESC for all Dubai Government entities (current version: ISR v3). 
LLM Large Language Model — an AI model trained on large datasets of text capable of 
generating and understanding natural language. 
OPEX Operational Expenditure — recurring annual costs for running, maintaining, and 
supporting the platform after go-live. 
PDPL UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45 of 2021 on the Protection of Personal Data — the 
UAE's primary data privacy law. 
RAG Retrieval-Augmented Generation — an AI architecture where a language model's 
outputs are grounded in real-time data retrieval, reducing hallucination. 
RBAC Role-Based Access Control — a security model where system access permissions are 
assigned based on defined user roles. 
RERA Real Estate Regulatory Agency — the regulatory arm of Dubai Land Department 
responsible for licensing developers and brokers, and managing the Escrow system.  
RPO Recovery Point Objective — the maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured 
in time following a system failure. 
RTO Recovery Time Objective — the maximum acceptable duration within which a 
system must be restored following a failure. 
RTL Right-to-Left — the text direction used by Arabic and Urdu, requiring full layout 
mirroring in user interface components. 
SLA Service Level Agreement — a contractual commitment defining the minimum 
performance and availability standards the vendor must maintain.

OneLake The single, unified data lake storage layer within Microsoft Fabric. DLD’s OneLake

encouraged to serve all platform visualisations and APIs from the Gold layer to 
maintain data consistency and avoid duplication. 
Silver / Gold Layers Data processing tiers within DLD’s OneLake Lakehouse architecture. The Silver 
Layer contains cleansed, validated, and conformed data transformed from raw source 
system ingestion. The Gold Layer contains aggregated, business-rule-applied, 
analytics-ready data sets that serve semantic models and all reporting and 
visualisation outputs. All IRETP platform visualisations and APIs must be served 
from the Gold Layer.
